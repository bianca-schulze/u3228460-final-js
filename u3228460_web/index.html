Six of Australia's Worst Fires

1. Black Sunday (VIC), 1 Feb-10 Mar 1926

Large areas of Gippsland caught fire, culminating in the Black Sunday fires on 14 February that killed 31 people in Warburton, near Melbourne. The fires affected Victorian communities in Stawell (Deep Lead), Yea, Moondarra, Grampians, Kinglake, and Anakie areas.

  • Burned area: 3900km2
  • Homes burned: 1000
  • Deaths: 60
  • Cost: Unknown

2. Black Tuesday (TAS), 7 Feb 1967

An unusually abundant spring covered Tasmanian forest floors with litter, providing excess fuel for the bushfire season. Strong northerly winds and high temperatures coupled to help fuel the fire, which swept over the south-east coast of the state and came within 2km of central Hobart.

  • Burned area: 2642km2
  • Homes burned: 1300
  • Deaths: 62
  • Cost: $101 million AUD

3. Black Friday (VIC), 13-20 Jan 1939

Drought conditions and constant water shortages also preceded Black Friday, but the usual combination of high temperatures, strong winds, and low humidity finally triggered fires throughout bush communities near Melbourne. Accidents and arsonists also started some of the fires.

  • Burned area: 20,000km2
  • Homes burned: 650
  • Deaths: 71
  • Cost: $250 million AUD

4. Ash Wednesday (VIC, SA), 16-18 Feb 1983

Drought, gale-force winds and high temperatures set the scene for a series of fires across Victoria and south-eastern South Australia. Accidents and arsonists started most of the fires, which spread rapidly through scenic residential regions near Melbourne.

  • Burned area: 2080 km2
  • Homes burned: 1900
  • Deaths: 75
  • Cost: $200 million AUD

5. Black Saturday (VIC), 7-8 Feb 2009

Black Saturday resulted from some of the worst fire conditions ever recorded in Victoria. Record-high temperatures and strong winds after a season of intense drought were contributing factors. Lots of public and private infrastructure were damaged or destroyed.

  • Burned area: 4500km2
  • Homes burned: 2000
  • Deaths: 173
  • Cost: $1 billion AUD

6. Black Summer, July 2019 – March 2020

High temperatures following years of repeated drought brought one of the worst bushfires seasons to ever strike. The Black Summer fires ravaged the Australian landscape, in NSW alone 5.4 million hectares burned sending recording breaking temperatures across the country.

  • Burned area: 243,000km2
  • Homes burned: 3,500
  • Deaths: 479
  • Cost: $78-88 billion AUD

Fire Safety

Scroll to see the three most important rules of fire safety

Tip 1: Install smoke alarms

A smoke alarm is critical for the early detection of a fire in your home and could mean the difference between life and death. Fires can occur in a variety of ways and in any room of your home. But no matter where or how, having a smoke alarm is the first key step toward your family’s safety.

Tip 2: Create a fire safety plan

Having a home escape plan in conjunction with a working smoke alarm will greatly increase your chances of getting out safely. Every second counts. As a minimum, your escape plan should include at least two exit points from every room and a safe meeting place outside.

Tip 3: Get down, get low, get out

In a fire, smoke and poisonous air hurt more people than the actual flames do. You'll breathe less smoke if you stay close to the ground. Smoke naturally rises, so if there is smoke while you're using your escape route, staying low means you can crawl under most of it.
